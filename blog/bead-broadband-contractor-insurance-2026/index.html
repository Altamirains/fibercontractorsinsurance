What Is the BEAD Program and Why Does It Matter for Installers?

The Broadband Equity, Access, and Deployment (BEAD) Program was created by the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA) to connect every unserved and underserved American household to high-speed internet. It is the single largest broadband infrastructure investment in U.S. history — and the bulk of the actual ground-level work will be done by fiber and cable installation contractors just like you.

Unlike previous broadband programs, BEAD requires states to move through a defined planning and approval process before releasing funds for construction. That process is now largely complete. The result: billions of dollars in subcontracts are about to hit the market simultaneously across the country starting in mid-2026.

💡 What This Means for Contractors

Prime contractors — the ISPs, CLECs, and large telecom companies receiving BEAD grants — will be sub-bidding enormous amounts of the physical installation work to smaller fiber and cable contractors. To be considered for that work, you need to show up with the right insurance documentation on day one. Contractors who aren't insured properly will be passed over, no exceptions.

Why Insurance Is a Hard Requirement for BEAD Subcontracts

BEAD is a federally funded program administered through state agencies. That means the insurance requirements layered into BEAD subcontracts aren't just from the prime contractor — they flow from federal grant compliance, state agency rules, and local ROW permit requirements all at once. There are typically three levels of insurance requirements you'll have to meet:

  • Prime Contractor Requirements: The ISP or telecom company holding the BEAD grant will require minimum insurance limits in their subcontractor agreements. These are often higher than standard commercial work — $2M general liability and $5M umbrella are common.
  • State Licensing Requirements: Your state contractor license (DOPL in Utah, NSCB in Nevada, CSLB in California, ROC in Arizona) requires active insurance and bonding. Let these lapse and you lose your license — and your ability to work on any BEAD project.
  • Local ROW Permits: BEAD projects that require digging in public rights-of-way trigger city and county permit insurance requirements. Municipalities require COIs naming them as additional insured before any trenching or aerial work begins.
⚠ Don't Wait Until You Win the Contract

Many contractors make the mistake of trying to get insured after they win a subcontract. By then it's too late. Prime contractors pre-qualify their subcontractor pool before contracts are awarded. If you're not already insured at the right limits, you won't be in the pool. Get your coverage in place now, before BEAD construction ramps up this summer.

Davis-Bacon Act: What Fiber Contractors Need to Know

BEAD projects with a total expected project cost of more than $5,000,000 trigger the Davis-Bacon Act, which requires contractors and subcontractors to pay locally prevailing wages and fringe benefits to all laborers and mechanics on the project.

For fiber and cable installers, this means:

  • Higher labor costs: Prevailing wages in California, Nevada, and urban Arizona are significantly higher than typical market rates. Build this into your bid.
  • Certified payroll requirements: You'll need to submit weekly certified payroll reports. Make sure your payroll system can handle this — it's a federal compliance requirement.
  • Workers' comp rates may increase: Higher wages mean higher workers' comp premium bases. Make sure your WC policy is rated for the correct wage levels when bidding BEAD work.
  • Recordkeeping: Davis-Bacon requires detailed records of worker classifications, hours, and wages for the life of the project plus three years.
📋 Note on Small BEAD Projects

Davis-Bacon only applies to projects over $5M in total cost. Many BEAD subcontracts — especially in rural Utah, Nevada, and Arizona — will be smaller than this threshold and won't trigger prevailing wage requirements. Check the total project cost in your subcontract agreement, not just your individual sub amount.

How to Get Your COI Ready Before BEAD Construction Starts

The window to get in front of BEAD prime contractors is right now — before construction starts this summer. Here's the action plan:

  • Step 1 — Audit your current coverage. Pull out your existing GL, WC, and auto policies. Check your limits. Most small cable and fiber contractors are underinsured for BEAD work — carrying $500K GL when prime contractors require $2M.
  • Step 2 — Get a quote for higher limits. Moving from $500K to $2M GL is often less expensive than contractors expect — sometimes just a few hundred dollars more per year. Get a quote now so you know your cost before bidding.
  • Step 3 — Make sure your state license is current. An expired DOPL, NSCB, CSLB, or ROC license disqualifies you from BEAD work immediately. Renew it now if it's coming up.
  • Step 4 — Get your COI template ready. Prime contractors will ask for a COI naming them as additional insured. Get your broker set up to issue these same-day so you don't lose a contract waiting for paperwork.
  • Step 5 — Register in your state's contractor directory. Several states including Minnesota have voluntary BEAD contractor directories. Check if your state has one and get listed — it's free visibility with prime contractors actively looking for subs.
